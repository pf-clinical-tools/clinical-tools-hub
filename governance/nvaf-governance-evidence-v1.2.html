Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool in Non-Valvular Atrial Fibrillation (NVAF)

Governance Rationale, Evidence Base, and Intended Use

Author: Dr Prabhath Fernando MBBS MD FRCP
Associated digital tool: Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool in NVAF (Version 1.2)
Intended setting: NHS secondary care and community practice
Intended users: Clinicians involved in the management of atrial fibrillation

1. Purpose and Scope

This document describes the clinical rationale, evidence base, governance considerations, and intended use of the Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool in Non-Valvular Atrial Fibrillation (NVAF).

The tool is designed to support clinicians in balancing stroke prevention and bleeding risk when considering oral anticoagulation (OAC) in patients with NVAF, particularly in older adults and those with frailty or comorbidity.

The tool:

  • Uses validated, guideline-endorsed risk scores (CHA₂DS₂-VASc and ORBIT) without modification.
  • Presents stroke and bleeding risks side-by-side to support structured clinical judgement.
  • Provides qualitative net clinical benefit statements rather than automated treatment recommendations.
  • Explicitly signals uncertainty and the need for individualised decision-making.

The tool does not replace clinical judgement, specialist advice, shared decision-making, or local and national guidance.

2. Clinical Background

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is a major risk factor for ischaemic stroke, with AF-related strokes associated with higher mortality and greater long-term disability than non-AF strokes. Oral anticoagulation substantially reduces stroke risk in NVAF but carries an inherent risk of bleeding, including major and intracranial haemorrhage.

Clinical decision-making in AF therefore requires balancing:

  • Absolute stroke risk without anticoagulation, and
  • Absolute bleeding risk with anticoagulation.

This balance is particularly challenging in:

  • Older adults
  • Patients with frailty
  • Those with renal impairment, anaemia, or prior bleeding
  • Patients with competing risks and limited physiological reserve

Guidelines consistently recommend assessing both stroke risk and bleeding risk, but also emphasise that bleeding risk scores should be used to identify modifiable risk factors, not as sole determinants to withhold anticoagulation.

In practice, clinicians must integrate:

  • Risk scores
  • Patient values and preferences
  • Frailty, cognition, falls risk, and comorbidity
  • Anticipated impact of stroke versus bleeding on quality of life

The NVAF Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool was developed to make this reasoning explicit, structured, and auditable, rather than implicit or inconsistent.

Representation of older and frail patients in clinical trials

Although oral anticoagulation is supported by robust evidence demonstrating stroke risk reduction in non-valvular atrial fibrillation, it is important to recognise that older adults and patients with frailty have been under-represented in many landmark anticoagulation trials.

Pivotal randomised controlled trials of warfarin and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) predominantly enrolled relatively fit older adults, with limited inclusion of patients with advanced frailty, multimorbidity, cognitive impairment, high falls risk, or limited life expectancy. As a result, trial populations may not fully reflect the complexity of patients encountered in routine clinical practice, particularly within secondary care and geriatric medicine settings.⁰

Consequently, estimates of absolute stroke reduction and bleeding risk derived from these trials represent population averages, and their direct applicability to frail individuals requires careful clinical interpretation. In frailty, the consequences of both stroke and major bleeding may be amplified, while competing risks and patient priorities may differ substantially from those represented in trial cohorts.

This recognised evidence gap underlines the importance of individualised decision-making, shared discussion with patients and carers, and cautious application of guideline recommendations in older and frail populations. The NVAF Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool is designed to support this nuanced judgement rather than to extrapolate trial findings uncritically to all patients.

3. Stroke Risk Assessment: CHA₂DS₂-VASc

The tool uses the CHA₂DS₂-VASc score to estimate annual stroke risk in patients with NVAF who are not anticoagulated.

CHA₂DS₂-VASc is:

  • Widely validated across diverse populations⁴
  • Recommended by major international guidelines⁵ ⁶
  • Simple to apply in routine clinical practice

Stroke risk estimates presented in the tool are derived from large cohort studies and meta-analyses and are expressed as approximate annual percentages, acknowledging population-level variability.⁷

In keeping with guideline recommendations:

  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc is used without modification
  • Sex-specific treatment thresholds are respected
  • The tool highlights that in frailty, even moderate absolute stroke risks may have disproportionately severe consequences

4. Bleeding Risk Assessment: ORBIT

4.1 Rationale for selecting ORBIT

Several bleeding risk scores exist, including HAS-BLED, ATRIA, and ORBIT. The present tool uses ORBIT for the following reasons:

  1. Predictive performance
    ORBIT has been shown to perform at least as well as, and in some studies better than, HAS-BLED for predicting major bleeding in anticoagulated AF patients.⁸
  2. Objective variables
    ORBIT relies predominantly on objective clinical variables (age, renal function, haemoglobin/anaemia, prior bleeding, antiplatelet therapy), reducing subjectivity and inter-observer variability.
  3. Simplicity and clinical usability
    ORBIT avoids labile INR and alcohol-use variables, making it particularly suitable in the era of DOACs and in settings where warfarin-specific metrics are less relevant.⁹
  4. Alignment with contemporary practice
    ORBIT was developed and validated in cohorts including patients treated with both warfarin and direct oral anticoagulants, making it applicable to modern anticoagulation strategies.⁸

For these reasons, ORBIT was selected as the bleeding risk estimator in this tool in preference to HAS-BLED, while recognising that no bleeding score perfectly predicts individual outcomes.

4.2 Governance principle

Consistent with guideline advice, ORBIT is used in this tool to:

  • Highlight bleeding risk
  • Identify modifiable factors
  • Inform the clinicianpatient discussion

It is not used to mandate withholding anticoagulation in isolation.

5. Rationale for Combined Presentation of Stroke and Bleeding Risk

Clinical guidelines consistently recommend assessing both stroke risk and bleeding risk when considering anticoagulation in NVAF. However, in routine practice these risks are often assessed sequentially or in isolation, rather than being considered together in a structured manner.

The NVAF Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool presents CHA₂DS₂-VASc and ORBIT outputs side-by-side, enabling clinicians to directly compare:

  • Absolute annual stroke risk without anticoagulation, and
  • Absolute annual major bleeding risk with anticoagulation.

Importantly, the tool does not calculate a single numerical net benefit score. There is currently no validated, guideline-endorsed method for combining stroke and bleeding risk scores into a single predictive metric for individual patients. Attempts to do so risk creating false precision and obscuring uncertainty.

Instead, the tool provides qualitative net clinical benefit statements that reflect common clinical reasoning patterns, explicitly signalling when decisions are:

  • strongly in favour of anticoagulation,
  • likely to favour anticoagulation with risk mitigation, or
  • uncertain and preference-sensitive.

This approach aligns with guideline emphasis on clinical judgement and shared decision-making rather than algorithmic decision-making.

6. Design Principles and Safety Features

The NVAF Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool was developed using explicit safety-by-design principles appropriate for clinical decision support.

6.1 Use of validated scores without modification

CHA₂DS₂-VASc and ORBIT are used unchanged, preserving their validated structure, thresholds, and interpretation.

6.2 Conservative language and uncertainty signalling

The tool deliberately avoids directive language. Outputs are framed using terms such as:

  • favours anticoagulation
  • uncertain; individualise
  • preference-sensitive

This avoids implying mandatory treatment decisions and reinforces clinician responsibility.

6.3 Explicit handling of uncertainty

Where stroke and bleeding risks are similar in magnitude, or where both risks are high, the tool explicitly labels the net clinical benefit as uncertain; individualise, prompting:

  • shared decision-making,
  • documentation of rationale,
  • consideration of patient values and goals of care.

6.4 Internal consistency checks

To prevent contradictory inputs:

  • Age ≥75 years is defined once within CHA₂DS₂-VASc and derived automatically for ORBIT.
  • This prevents logically inconsistent age band selection across risk scores and reduces user error.

6.5 Reset functionality

A reset function is included to:

  • Clear all inputs between assessments
  • Reduce risk of cross-patient data contamination
  • Support safe use in busy clinical environments

7. Interpretation and Clinical Use

The tool is intended to be used as part of a structured clinical assessment, not as a standalone decision-maker.

Clinicians should:

  • Use the output to inform discussion with patients and carers
  • Consider frailty, cognition, falls risk, comorbidity, and life expectancy
  • Address modifiable bleeding risk factors where possible
  • Document the reasoning behind anticoagulation decisions, particularly where uncertainty exists

The tool may be particularly helpful in:

  • Older adults with intermediate stroke risk
  • Patients with prior bleeding or anaemia
  • Situations where clinicians experience equipoise or disagreement

8. Limitations

This tool has several important limitations:

  • Risk estimates are derived from population-level data and do not predict individual outcomes.
  • Frailty is heterogeneous and not fully captured by existing risk scores.
  • Competing risks and non-cardiovascular mortality are not modelled.
  • The tool does not replace specialist input in complex cases.

These limitations are inherent to current evidence and are explicitly acknowledged within the tools design.

9. Digital Clinical Safety and Governance Considerations

The NVAF Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool is a clinical decision support tool, not a medical device that automates treatment decisions.

It is intended to:

  • Reduce unwarranted variation
  • Promote transparent reasoning
  • Support documentation and governance review

Version control is maintained, and substantive changes are expected to be driven by emerging evidence or guideline updates rather than cosmetic iteration.

10. Conclusion

The Anticoagulation Risk Assessment Tool in Non-Valvular Atrial Fibrillation (NVAF) has been developed to support clinicians in navigating the complex balance between stroke prevention and bleeding risk, particularly in older adults and patients with frailty, where decision-making is often nuanced and preference-sensitive.

By combining validated, guideline-endorsed risk scores (CHA₂DS₂-VASc and ORBIT) without modification and presenting their outputs side-by-side, the tool promotes structured clinical reasoning while avoiding false precision or algorithmic decision-making. Its deliberate use of conservative language, explicit uncertainty signalling, and safety features reflects both the limitations of the current evidence base and the realities of contemporary clinical practice.

The tool is intended to support, not replace, clinical judgement, shared decision-making, and appropriate documentation. Used within appropriate clinical governance frameworks, it may help reduce unwarranted variation, enhance transparency of decision-making, and improve the quality of clinicianpatient discussions regarding anticoagulation in NVAF.
